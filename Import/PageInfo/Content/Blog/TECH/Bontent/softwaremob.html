Terminology/Reason

2 Min. Read

Open Source: Open Source is generally free and the code is public; meaning you will usually have full access to the program's code.

Licensing: Proprietary Software (not Open Source), such as Windows/Apple and other big name brand Softwares/Services.

Perpetual

I do not lease software nor do I purchase subscriptions; unless they fall into specific niches, such as: Email, VPN, Hosting, and the like. I will never spend money on general software that I find myself rationalizing the reasons as to why I need a specific tool or program, at a specific cost/ratio.

Leasing

Leasing is normally a perpetual license. In many cases it grants access to future updates (read the terms of software). Sometimes these updates are time sensitive; again read the terms of the software.

Subscription

This is 100% time sensitive.

Pros/Cons

The pros and cons of my own method (Perpetual/OpenSource) are as follows.

Cons

1. Locks you into a particular version.

2. Much more expensive upfront.

3. Updates and new features will expire upon the release of a new version.


Pros

1. Security.

2. No Subscription.

3. Own your copy.


The choice will depend on what you want to accomplish and your projects. Not all are logical selections for everyone and it will vary depending on your particular needs.


Cost

I hold the opinion that, if you use a software as a main tool, then you should support that company.

Perpetual licencing is fantastic for well built software, longevity, and solid computer life.


Ratio

A prime example of this is Adobe Products. The only REAL difference between Adobe CS6 and the newest version (CC:Creative Cloud) is the name, the Cloud, and the fact that it is now ONLY subscription based.


Perpetual licencing has kept me from having to deal with that nightmare.

I have all the perks of Adobe- without the forced updates and without granting corporate access to my devices in order to use their products. Keep in mind that these products are bundled with a ton of things you will never need or want. This turns solid software into Bloatware- and, in most cases, you pay for that bloat.


Not all updates are good. Subscriptions make you dependent. Leasing software has you completely at the mercy of corporations in many instances.


Do not be a slave.

I encourage you all to support developments. I must point out that there is a difference between supporting and being extorted.

Be mindful of this as you review your budget and begin making choices of how you want to license the product. It is entirely up to you- UNLESS Adobe, Microsoft, Avid, etc. say otherwise.

It does seem expensive for perpetual licenses to cost anywhere between $50-$600!

Currently, the majority of subscriptions start at $120/year (Basic: $10/month) and can be as much as $953/year (Pro: $80/month)!

Which one is cheaper?
Cost/Ratio= Perpetual licencing everytime.


If I did not have a perpetual license before Adobe switched to "Cloud Subscription" 10 years ago- well, you would have to multiply that total by $120 for the basic packages (1/4 of what I currently own). This doubles the price and a payment is due again next month.

Having a perpetual license ends up being much cheaper and it also means I own the software (Pro Package). Adobe can take that cloud nonsense elsewhere!

I moved on and broke down each Software to dedicated Flash Drives with all the required drivers (etc.) before moving to make dual copies of each to CDs and then duplicates.

Even investing in a separate tiny dedicated server to access via the network if I ever had need for it.


This may all seem overboard or crazy to many, but now with Win 10 all that redundant backing up has really paid off.

Reinstall

I had to update one of my main computers not too long ago- not a problem. Without the backups this would have been a nightmare and in some cases impossible. Instead, I had every boot option and installer option possible.

That will make more sense to AMD users. Even more sense to those who, like me, have an AMD machine (I prefer for Graphics) running Win 7.
